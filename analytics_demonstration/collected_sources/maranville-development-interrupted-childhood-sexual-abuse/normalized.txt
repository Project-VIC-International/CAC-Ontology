When Development Is Interrupted by Childhood Sexual Abuse
Author: Melissa Maranville, PhD
Published: 2026-02-05
Source URL: https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/when-development-interrupted-childhood-sexual-abuse-maranville-phd-wlrje/

Much of how trauma is discussed assumes a child first develops a sense of safety, identity, and connection, and that trauma later disrupts those foundations. This framework does not apply when sexual abuse occurs in early childhood.
When childhood sexual abuse begins early, it does not interrupt a finished system. It shapes development while it is still forming.
Child development follows identifiable stages. During early and middle childhood, children are learning who they are, whether other people are safe, whether their body belongs to them, and whether closeness brings comfort or danger. These lessons are learned through experience, not instruction.
Beginning in early childhood, around ages four to five, and continuing through middle childhood into early adolescence, children develop the basic skills needed for relationships. This is when they learn how to make friends, trust others, regulate emotions, and feel emotionally safe with others. These capacities are built through repeated experiences of safety and consistency.
When development is interrupted by childhood sexual abuse during this critical period, the process does not pause. It is altered.
Instead of learning safety, the child learns threat. Instead of learning trust, the child learns vigilance. Instead of learning that closeness brings comfort, the body learns that closeness brings danger.
Psychological research describes this pattern as developmental trauma or complex trauma. Unlike a single traumatic event that occurs after development is established, childhood sexual abuse happens while the brain and nervous system are still forming. The trauma does not occur to a finished identity. It shapes the system itself.
Example narrative: first memory of sexual abuse occurred at age four or five; the abuse continued through key developmental years until approximately age eleven.
The developmental stage in which friendship, trust, and emotional safety are typically formed was disrupted rather than delayed.
That disruption has remained consistent into adulthood. Research shows that attachment patterns formed in early childhood are relatively stable across the lifespan. While therapy and insight can improve functioning, they do not recreate developmental stages that were missed or damaged.
This is why the language of healing is often misleading when applied to survivors of childhood sexual abuse. Healing is frequently described as recovery or returning to a previous state. For individuals whose identity and attachment developed under chronic threat, there is no baseline of safety to restore.
As a society, we also struggle to name childhood sexual abuse clearly. Broader terms such as trafficking, assault, or rape are often used because they create distance. That same distancing appears in language like child prostitute or young woman, which shifts responsibility away from the harm and minimizes the reality that the victim was a child.
Avoiding clear language does not protect children. It protects adult comfort and institutional stability.
Because childhood sexual abuse shapes development itself, it is critical that survivors are able to speak openly and be believed. Asking survivors to remain silent, forget, or minimize their experiences does not reduce trauma. It compounds it.
Childhood sexual abuse is one of the most quiet and deeply traumatizing experiences a child can endure. Its effects do not disappear when ignored. They shape lives when denied.
Truth is not harmful. Silence is.
